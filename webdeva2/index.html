The history of digital computers

  • Pre 1937

    Before 1937 there were many attempts to make machines that process data. The earliest example of this would be the abacus a tool used to help people perform calculations .However these machines are not really computers as computers as being electronic and able to store data. However, there was one concept machine. That was never finished called the Babbage difference engine. It was designed and partially built during the 1820s and ’30s by Charles Babbage. This design was considered to be the father of all computers.

  • 1937

    Bell Laboratories scientist George Stibitz uses relays to make an adder. Called the “Model K” Adder because he built it on his “Kitchen” table, this simple demonstration circuit provides proof of concept for applying Boolean logic to the design of computers, resulting in construction of the relay-based Model I Complex Calculator in 1939. That same year in Germany, engineer Konrad Zuse built his Z2 computer, also using telephone company relays.

  • 1942

    In 1942 the first electronic was made. The computer was called the Atanasoff–Berry computer(ABC). It was built by physics Professor John Vincent Atanasoff and his graduate student, Clifford Berry, in 1942 at Iowa State College, now known as Iowa State University. The ABC weighed over 700 pounds and used vacuum tubes. It had a rotating drum, a little bigger than a paint can, that had small capacitors on it.

  • 1941

    In 1941 Konrad Zuse finishes the Z3 computer. The Z3, an early computer built by German engineer Konrad Zuse working in complete isolation from developments elsewhere, uses 2,300 relays, performs floating point binary arithmetic, and has a 22-bit word length. The Z3 was used for aerodynamic calculations but was destroyed in a bombing raid on Berlin in late 1943. Zuse later supervised a reconstruction of the Z3 in the 1960s, which is currently on display at the Deutsches Museum in Munich.
